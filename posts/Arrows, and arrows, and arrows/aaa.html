Arrows, and arrows, and arrows...

Problem

Uncle Qiang's house consists of a $2026 \times 2026$ grid of $1 \times 1$ rooms. Every room has four walls, exactly one of which contains an outgoing one way door. If the wall is between two adjacent rooms, then the door leads from one room to the other. If the wall is on the perimeter of the house then the door leads outside. In every room, each of the four walls has at most one door, which leads either into or out of the room. There are no incoming doors from outside of the house.

Uncle Qiang wants to redesign the house by performing moves: in a move, he chooses a room whose four walls are not all occupied by doors. He then removes that room's outgoing one-way door and replaces it with a new outgoing one-way door through one of its walls not occupied by a door.

Uncle Qiang would like to perform a series of up to $k$ moves so that it is possible to exit the house from any room by following only outgoing one-way doors. Find the smallest number $k$ such that this is always possible. This problem was from the IMSC 2026 Mock IMO Day 1, Problem 1.

Solution

I'd like to preface this by saying that this problem is an absolute yap fest. Anyways, I was able to (slightly) easily solve this due to the resemblance of this problem with Nepal TST 2025/5, so there's that I guess(I'm very experienced with drawing arrows now).

We claim that the answer is $1013^2$. In general, for any $n \times n$ board where $n$ is even, the answer will be $\left( \frac n2 \right)^2$. Obviously, like a gangster, we prove the statement for general $n$.

For the construction, we consider a $2\times2$ subgrid where going through any room leads to a repeating cycle of going through the four rooms. Since $n$ is even, we can fill the $n\times n$ board with such squares. Obviously, to stop the cycle, we must apply a move to at least one of the four doors, leaving us with a total of $\frac 14 \left( n \right)^2$ moves, or $\left( \frac n2 \right)^2$ moves. All right folks, now on to the annoying part!

We now show that this is the minimum required. Consider only the outgoing doors of each room, and denote by it an arrow that points to the room it leads to. Let the four cardinal directions up, down, left, and right be where the arrows point to. Note that there exists two directions which appear at most $\left( \frac n2 \right)^2$ times. WLOG, let us consider one of the them to be the doors pointing up, then flip them such that they now point down. Uncle Qiang can perform this columnwise without an issue.

Now since we have removed all ways to go up, Uncle Qiang can only go down. If, he does not escape already, then that means he must reach a point where he can only go left or right. This solves the problem, as there cannot be two arrows facing each other in adjacent rooms(thus blocking him from exiting), so he can just follow one of the directions to exit the grid, we're done.
